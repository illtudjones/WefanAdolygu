# Ethical, Legal and Environmental Impacts

## Ethical impacts of digital technology

- **Privacy** — computers collect and store vast amounts of personal data. Users often do not know what data is collected or how it is used.
- **Cybersecurity** — technology creates new ways to commit crimes (fraud, identity theft, hacking) and raises questions about who is responsible for protecting people.
- **Automation and employment** — computers and robots replace some jobs while creating others. This raises questions about fairness and retraining workers.
- **Artificial intelligence** — AI systems can make decisions affecting people's lives (e.g. credit scoring, job applications). Bias in training data can produce unfair outcomes.
- **Digital divide** — not everyone has equal access to technology or internet connectivity, increasing inequality.
- **Surveillance** — governments and companies can monitor people's online activity and physical movements. This conflicts with the right to privacy.

Professionals in computing should follow a **code of conduct** (formal or informal) that guides ethical behaviour, such as the British Computer Society (BCS) Code of Conduct.

## Legislation

### Data Protection Act 1998 (updated by GDPR / Data Protection Act 2018)
- Controls how organisations collect, store and use personal data.
- Key principles: data must be fairly and lawfully obtained, used only for the stated purpose, accurate and up to date, not kept longer than necessary, and kept secure.
- Individuals have the right to see data held about them.

### Computer Misuse Act 1990
- Makes it illegal to access a computer system without authorisation (hacking).
- Three offences: unauthorised access, unauthorised access with intent to commit further crime, and unauthorised modification of computer material (including installing malware).

### Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988
- Protects the intellectual property of software developers and content creators.
- Copying or distributing software without a licence is illegal.
- Open-source licences allow free use and modification under specific conditions.

### Freedom of Information Act 2000
- Gives people the right to request information held by public bodies (e.g. government, councils, NHS).
- Promotes transparency and accountability.

### Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000 (RIPA)
- Governs how authorities can intercept communications and conduct surveillance.
- Balances security needs against individuals' right to privacy.

## Environmental issues

### Energy consumption
- Data centres that store and process internet data use enormous amounts of electricity.
- Streaming video, cloud services and cryptocurrency mining all have large carbon footprints.
- Energy-efficient hardware design and renewable energy reduce this impact.

### Electronic waste (e-waste)
- Discarded computers, phones and other devices contain toxic materials (lead, mercury, cadmium).
- Improper disposal contaminates soil and water.
- Solutions include recycling schemes and designing products to last longer.

### Manufacturing
- Making hardware requires rare earth minerals, often mined with significant environmental and human cost.
- Planned obsolescence — designing products to fail or become outdated — increases waste.

### Positive environmental uses
- Technology can monitor and reduce environmental damage (smart grids, pollution sensors, precision agriculture).
- Remote working reduces commuting and office energy use.
